- **The Specialists:** _Thiobacillus_ (Sulfur), _Methylobacterium_ (Methanol), and the N-fixers (_Azotobacter/Azospirillum_) are identified by their **exclusive growth** on their respective selective media.
    
- **The Morphological Outlier:** _Streptomyces_ is the only one in this list that forms a true mycelium. While it grows on Starch Agar, its morphology is the primary "differential" factor.
    
- **The "Look-Alikes":** * **Bacillus vs. Paenibacillus:** These are very similar. Differentiation usually requires a **Voges-Proskauer (VP) test** or specific sugar fermentations, but they are grouped together as endospore-formers on standard media.
    
    - **Stenotrophomonas vs. Pseudomonas:** _Pseudomonas_ is usually Oxidase positive and fluorescent on King's B; _Stenotrophomonas_ is Oxidase negative and utilizes maltose aggressively.
        
    - **Paraburkholderia:** Often requires **BCSA (Burkholderia cepacia selective agar)** to truly separate it from _Pseudomonas_ in a complex sample, but in this specific list, its growth patterns on MacConkey (non-lactose fermenting) and lack of fluorescence on King's B help isolate it.

### Step 1: The Physical/Chemical "Hammer" (Pre-treatment)

Before you even touch a petri dish, you use the unique physical properties of certain genera to kill off the background "noise" (like _E. coli_ or common _Staph_).

- **Heat Shock (80°C for 10-15 mins):** This is the gold standard for isolating **Bacillus** and **Paenibacillus**. It kills almost all vegetative cells but triggers the germination of their endospores.
    
- **Acid Wash (pH 3.0):** If you are looking for **Thiobacillus**, washing your sample in a low-pH sulfuric acid solution will eliminate 99% of common soil bacteria that cannot survive extreme acidity.
    
- **Air-Drying (Desiccation):** **Streptomyces** and **Azotobacter** (cysts) survive drying much better than most Gram-negative rods. Drying your soil sample for 48 hours helps "sink" the background population.
    

---

### Step 2: The Metabolic "Gatekeeper" (Enrichment)

You place your treated sample into liquid media that contains "poison" for most bacteria but "food" for your target.

- **For Methylobacterium:** Use a broth where **Methanol** is the _only_ carbon source. Almost nothing else can eat it; the "unnecessary" microbes simply starve.
    
- **For Azotobacter/Azospirillum:** Use a **Nitrogen-free** broth. If a microbe can't pull nitrogen out of the air (N-fixation), it cannot grow.
    
- **For Thiobacillus:** Use an inorganic salts medium with **elemental sulfur**. This effectively locks out all heterotrophs (microbes that need organic carbon/sugar).
### The "Secret Sauce": Selective Antibiotics

In a lab setting, we often "cheat" by adding specific antibiotics to the media to suppress the specific background microbes we know are in the sample:

1. **Cycloheximide:** Added to almost all these plates to stop **fungi** from overgrowing the bacteria.
    
2. **Penicillin:** Often used at low doses to isolate **Pseudomonas** or **Stenotrophomonas**, which have natural resistance (efflux pumps) that common bacteria lack.
    
3. **Nitrofurantoin:** Used to select for **Stenotrophomonas** while killing off _Pseudomonas_.
